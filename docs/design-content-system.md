# Purpose

Every future page should look and read as if it belongs to the same product.

The purpose of this system is consistency rather than creativity for its own sake. New pages should reuse established structures, visual patterns, terminology, and publishing standards. Individual topics may differ, but the reader should always understand where they are, what kind of content they are viewing, and how it relates to the rest of the website.

# Website Architecture

## Home

Introduces Misha Slipych, establishes the Engineering–Visualization–Automation positioning, presents key experience signals, and directs visitors toward the most relevant work.

## Portfolio

Provides the primary overview of engineering work.

### Projects

Projects summarize tools, systems, visual workflows, and documentation work. They should help visitors scan capabilities quickly and decide which work they want to explore in more detail.

## Case Studies

Explain selected projects in depth. Case studies should show the engineering context, decisions, constraints, process, and verified results behind the finished work.

## Articles

Publish focused explanations of engineering methods, visualization workflows, automation concepts, and practical lessons. Articles should remain useful independently of any single project.

## About

Explains professional focus, working approach, relevant disciplines, and the connection between engineering, visualization, and automation.

## Contact

Provides a direct path for relevant project discussions, professional opportunities, and technical collaboration.

# Content Hierarchy

## Project

A concise record of a specific tool, system, workflow, visualization, or documentation deliverable. A project page answers what it is, why it exists, what it does, which technologies it uses, and its current status.

## Case Study

A detailed examination of a selected project. It explains the problem, engineering context, constraints, decisions, process, tradeoffs, challenges, and verified outcomes. Use a case study only when enough approved material exists to explain the work properly.

## Article

A standalone piece of professional knowledge. Articles explain methods, patterns, concepts, or recurring engineering problems without depending on one project. They should teach something transferable.

## Technical Note

A shorter, narrowly scoped record of an observation, calculation, test, implementation detail, or workflow decision. Technical notes are appropriate when the subject is useful but does not require a full article.

# Writing Principles

- Be concise.
- Write engineering-first.
- Use factual, supportable statements.
- Avoid marketing language and inflated claims.
- Explain important engineering decisions.
- Explain tradeoffs and rejected alternatives when relevant.
- Explain lessons learned and what could be reused.
- Use diagrams whenever they communicate the system more clearly than prose.
- Define specialist terms when the intended reader may not know them.
- Separate verified results from estimates, assumptions, and future plans.

# Project Card Rules

Every project card should contain:

- Title
- One-sentence summary
- Technologies
- Status
- Thumbnail
- Clear call to action

Keep card summaries comparable in length. Use the same status vocabulary across the site. A call to action must describe a real available destination; do not imply that an unpublished page is accessible.

# Image Rules

## Preferred Aspect Ratios

- Hero: **16:9**
- Project thumbnails: **4:3**
- Social: **1200 × 630**
- Portrait: **4:5**

## Visual Direction

- Use dark backgrounds.
- Maintain consistent lighting across related images.
- Use orange as a restrained accent rather than a dominant fill.
- Preserve industrial realism and technically plausible geometry.
- Keep compositions clear and free from unnecessary visual clutter.
- Use approved, optimized production assets with descriptive alt text.

# Video Rules

Preferred delivery formats:

- MP4
- WebM

Videos should be:

- Loop-friendly when used as ambient or explanatory motion.
- Muted when autoplay is enabled.
- Optimized for the intended viewport and delivery context.
- Supplied with a static poster image where loading time or accessibility requires one.

Use autoplay only where appropriate. Never autoplay video with sound. Provide controls when the video contains information that cannot be understood from a short loop.

# Naming Conventions

- Use kebab-case for files, folders, media, and URLs.
- Keep project slugs short, descriptive, and permanent.
- Never rename published URLs.
- Include the project or article slug at the start of related media filenames.
- Use numbered suffixes only when several assets share the same role.

# Publishing Checklist

Before publishing every page, verify:

- Engineering accuracy
- Confidentiality and client approval
- Image and video quality
- Accessibility, including headings, keyboard use, alt text, captions, and contrast
- Page title, description, canonical URL, Open Graph, and Twitter metadata
- Appropriate structured data
- Internal links and related-content links
- Responsive layout on desktop and mobile
- Asset optimization and loading behavior
- Sitemap inclusion when the page becomes public

# Long-term Vision

The website should develop into an engineering knowledge base rather than remain only a portfolio of completed work.

The goal is to build:

- Credibility
- Discoverability
- Reusable content
- Long-term professional reputation

Projects demonstrate capability. Case studies explain engineering judgment. Articles and technical notes turn individual experience into knowledge that remains useful over time. Each form of content should strengthen the others.

The objective is not simply to showcase finished work. It is to create a coherent, durable body of engineering knowledge.

**Every new page should make the website stronger than it was before.**
